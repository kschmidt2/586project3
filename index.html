The Changing Religious Landscape in the United States

Oct. 28, 2015
  • Introduction
  • About the Data
The share of Americans who identify as Christian is declining while the number of American adults who do not identify with any organized religion is growing, according to a 2014 study from the Pew Research Center. These changes are occurring across the country and while the decline is especially pronounced in the younger generations, it is taking place among all age groups. The United States is still home to more Christians than any other country as roughly one in seven Americans identifies with some branch of Christianity, but the 2014 survey found the percentage of adults who identify as Christian dropped eight percentage points in the seven years since a similar Pew study in 2007. View the graphs below to see results from the 2014 study and compare how Americans identified in 2007 with how they identified in 2014.
The centerpiece of the 2014 U.S. Religious Landscape Study was a nationally representative telephone survey of 35,071 adults. It was a follow up to the Pew Research Center's 2007 religious survey of over 35,000 Americans. The latest survey was conducted in both English and Spanish from June 4-Sept. 30, 2014. The survey is estimated to cover 97% of the U.S. population and had a margin of sampling error of plus or minus 0.6 points. The "change" represented in the graphs below represent only statistically significant changes, blank cells indicate the difference between 2007 an 2014 is within this margin. Even this small margin of error when applied to hundreds of millions of Americans can yield a wide range of estimates for the size of particular faiths.
